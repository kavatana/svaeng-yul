## Demography (10)

### q-demography-001 · Population Structure · _easy_

A population pyramid displays a population broken down by which two characteristics?

- **A.** Income and education
- **B.** Age and sex ✅
- **C.** Religion and language
- **D.** Urban and rural residence

**Why:** A population pyramid is a back-to-back bar chart showing the number of people in each age group, split by sex (males on one side, females on the other), so it reveals the age–sex structure at a glance. Income and education (A) and religion and language (C) are social characteristics not shown on a standard pyramid, and urban–rural residence (D) is a separate geographic breakdown.

_Source: Public-health demography (foundations)_

### q-demography-002 · Child Mortality · _easy_

The infant mortality rate measures deaths among children in which age range, per 1,000 live births?

- **A.** Under 1 year ✅
- **B.** Under 5 years
- **C.** Under 10 years
- **D.** Ages 1 to 4 years

**Why:** The infant mortality rate counts deaths of infants before their first birthday (under 1 year) per 1,000 live births in a year. Under-5 deaths (B) are captured by the separate under-five mortality rate, and the 1–4 year band (D) describes child mortality after infancy. Under 10 years (C) is not a standard demographic indicator.

_Source: CDHS 2021–22_

### q-demography-003 · Fertility Trends · _easy_

The total fertility rate (TFR) is best described as the average number of children:

- **A.** Born in a country in a single year
- **B.** A woman would have over her reproductive lifetime at current rates ✅
- **C.** Living in an average household
- **D.** Who survive to age five

**Why:** TFR estimates the average number of children a woman would bear over her lifetime if she experienced the current age-specific fertility rates, so it is a per-woman lifetime measure. It is not an annual national count (A) or an average household size (C), and survival to age five (D) is a child-mortality concept rather than a fertility measure.

_Source: Public-health demography (foundations)_

### q-nursing-001 · WHO Hand Hygiene · _easy_

According to WHO, which action is the single most effective measure to prevent healthcare-associated infections?

- **A.** Wearing gloves continuously
- **B.** Hand hygiene ✅
- **C.** Routine antibiotics for all patients
- **D.** Isolating every patient

**Why:** WHO identifies hand hygiene as the single most effective, evidence-based action to reduce transmission of healthcare-associated infections. Wearing gloves continuously (A) does not replace hand hygiene and can spread organisms if gloves are not changed, routine antibiotics (C) drive resistance rather than prevent spread, and isolating every patient (D) is neither feasible nor the primary measure.

Hand hygiene 'before a clean or aseptic procedure' (Moment 2) is performed mainly to:

### q-nursing-006 · Patient Safety · _medium_

Before giving a medication, the safest first step to confirm you have the right patient is to:

- **A.** Ask the patient in the next bed

- **C.** Rely on the bed number alone

Asking a neighbouring patient (A) is unreliable, the bed number alone (C) fails if patients move, and matching by appearance (D) is error-prone, so each increases the risk of a wrong-patient error.

### q-nursing-007 · Infection Prevention · _medium_

'Standard precautions' in infection prevention are best described as measures applied:

- **A.** Only to patients with a confirmed infection
- **B.** To all patients regardless of known infection status ✅
- **C.** Only inside operating theatres
- **D.** Only during declared outbreaks

**Why:** Standard precautions are applied to every patient at all times, treating all blood and body fluids as potentially infectious, because infection status is often unknown. Restricting them to confirmed infections (A), to theatres (C), or to outbreaks (D) would leave many real exposures unprotected, which is exactly what the universal 'standard' approach is designed to avoid.

### q-infection-001 · Dengue Vector · _easy_

Dengue is caused by the dengue virus and is transmitted to humans mainly by:

- **A.** Anopheles mosquitoes
- **B.** Aedes mosquitoes ✅
- **C.** Houseflies
- **D.** Freshwater snails

**Why:** Dengue virus is transmitted mainly by Aedes mosquitoes (notably Aedes aegypti), which bite during the day. Anopheles mosquitoes (A) transmit malaria, not dengue; houseflies (C) are linked to faecal–oral spread of other pathogens; and freshwater snails (D) are intermediate hosts for flukes, not dengue vectors.

_Source: Standard microbiology_

### q-infection-002 · Malaria Agent · _easy_

Malaria in humans is caused by which type of organism?

- **A.** A virus
- **B.** A bacterium
- **C.** A Plasmodium parasite ✅
- **D.** A fungus

**Why:** Malaria is caused by single-celled Plasmodium parasites (protozoa), transmitted by Anopheles mosquitoes. It is not a virus (A) like dengue, not a bacterium (B) like the agent of tuberculosis, and not a fungus (D). Correctly classifying the agent is essential to understanding transmission and prevention.

_Source: Standard microbiology_

**Why:** The three primary germ layers are ectoderm, mesoderm, and endoderm, so mesoderm is correct. 'Periderm' (A) is not one of the three layers, the myometrium (C) is the muscular wall of the uterus, and the trophoblast (D) contributes to the placenta rather than being one of the embryonic germ layers.

_Source: Standard human embryology_

### q-embryology-004 · Blastocyst · _medium_

The blastocyst has an outer cell layer and an inner cell mass. The inner cell mass mainly gives rise to:

- **A.** The placenta only
- **B.** The embryo proper ✅
- **C.** The uterine lining
- **D.** The mother's blood vessels

**Why:** The inner cell mass (embryoblast) gives rise to the embryo proper, while the outer trophoblast contributes to the placenta. So 'placenta only' (A) actually describes the trophoblast, the uterine lining (C) is maternal endometrium, and maternal blood vessels (D) are the mother's tissue — none of which is the fate of the inner cell mass.

_Source: Standard human embryology_

### q-embryology-005 · Implantation · _medium_

Around the end of the first week, the blastocyst normally begins to implant into the:

- **A.** Endometrium of the uterus ✅
- **B.** Muscular myometrium
- **C.** Cervical canal
- **D.** Wall of the fallopian tube

**Why:** Normal implantation occurs in the endometrium, the inner lining of the uterus, which is prepared to receive the blastocyst. The myometrium (B) is the deeper muscle layer, the cervical canal (C) is the lower outlet, and implantation in the fallopian tube wall (D) would be an abnormal (ectopic) site — so the endometrium is the correct normal location.

_Source: Standard human embryology_

### q-embryology-006 · Gametogenesis · _medium_

Gametogenesis differs from ordinary body-cell division because it uses meiosis to produce cells that are:

- **A.** Genetically identical to the parent cell
- **B.** Haploid (carrying half the chromosome number) ✅
- **C.** Polyploid (carrying extra chromosome sets)
- **D.** Completely without any chromosomes

**Why:** Gametogenesis uses meiosis to produce haploid gametes carrying half the chromosome number, so that fertilization restores the normal diploid count. Genetically identical cells (A) result from mitosis, polyploid cells (C) carry abnormal extra sets, and gametes are not without chromosomes (D) — each carries a single set.

_Source: Standard human embryology_

### q-embryology-007 · Neurulation · _medium_

During neurulation, the neural tube — the future brain and spinal cord — forms from which germ layer?

- **A.** Endoderm
- **B.** Mesoderm
- **C.** Ectoderm ✅
- **D.** Trophoblast

**Why:** The neural tube forms from ectoderm, which also gives rise to the epidermis. Endoderm (A) forms the gut lining and associated organs, mesoderm (B) forms muscle, bone, and the circulatory system, and the trophoblast (D) is placental tissue — so only ectoderm gives rise to the nervous system.

_Source: Standard human embryology_

### q-embryology-008 · Bilaminar Disc · _hard_

By the end of the second week, the embryo is described as a 'bilaminar disc'. This means it consists of:

- **A.** A single uniform layer of cells
- **B.** Two layers — epiblast and hypoblast ✅
- **C.** Three established germ layers
- **D.** A hollow ball with no layers

**Why:** At the end of week two the embryonic disc is bilaminar, composed of two layers — epiblast and hypoblast. A single uniform layer (A) precedes this stage, three established germ layers (C) appear later during gastrulation (a trilaminar disc), and a hollow ball with no layers (D) describes the earlier blastocyst — so 'two layers' is the defining feature here.

_Source: Standard human embryology_

### q-embryology-009 · Gastrulation · _hard_

Gastrulation is a critical developmental step primarily because it:

- **A.** Marks the moment of fertilization
- **B.** Establishes the three primary germ layers that form all tissues ✅
- **C.** Completes formation of the placenta
- **D.** Triggers the onset of labour

**Why:** Gastrulation transforms the bilaminar disc into a trilaminar disc, establishing ectoderm, mesoderm, and endoderm — the three germ layers from which all later tissues and organs arise. It is not fertilization (A), which occurs much earlier, nor placental completion (C) or the onset of labour (D), which are unrelated events; its significance is germ-layer formation.

_Source: Standard human embryology_

### q-embryology-010 · Teratogen Timing · _hard_

Exposure to a teratogen is generally most likely to cause major structural birth defects during which period?

- **A.** Before fertilization
- **B.** The embryonic period (roughly weeks 3–8), when organs are forming ✅
- **C.** Only in the final week before birth
- **D.** Only after birth

**Why:** The embryonic period (roughly weeks 3–8) is the time of organogenesis, when developing structures are most sensitive to teratogens, so major structural defects are most likely then. Before fertilization (A) there is no embryo to affect, while the final week before birth (C) and after birth (D) fall well past the main organ-forming window and carry lower risk for major structural anomalies.

_Source: Standard human embryology_
